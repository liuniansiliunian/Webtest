This is a year collection

As the curtain draws to a close on 2024, I find myself standing at the precipice of a new year, reflecting on the tapestry of experiences that have woven together the story of my past twelve months. This annual record serves as a chronicle of my personal odyssey, capturing the essence of my triumphs, the depths of my struggles, and the joy of my accomplishments.

In the pages that follow, I document some of pieces of time that have defined my 2024. Each entry is a snapshot of time, a testament to the days when I pushed boundaries, learned new lessons, and celebrated the small victories that collectively make up the grand narrative of my life. Join me as I take you through the highs and lows, the insights and the lessons learned, and the memories that have made 2024 a year to remember.

Look Back

Actually a movie review.

Fujimoto Tatsuki and Oshiya Kiyotaka have crafted a love letter to comic and animation creators, as well as to the countless supporters who push them forward.

Read more
Full Article

"Look Back" is a true masterpiece that redefines the potential of Japanese animation. Among this year's strong lineup of TV anime and their feature films, none can match the production quality of this work, which is essentially a product of commercial compromise. The credit goes not only to the original work by Fujimoto Tatsuki but also to the delicate touch of the director and the rest of the production team. For a detailed production analysis, one can refer to NBHT's video from a few months ago. Instead of repeating that, let's discuss the connection and differences between "motion and comics."

The film's strong cinematic style is a main feature of Fujimoto's work, especially in its storyboarding. This not only reduces the difficulty of adaptation but also makes it easier to showcase the original flavor. The film remains faithful to the original, from its storyboards and classic fixed shots to the detailed backgrounds, dialogue pauses, and even Fujimoto's dynamic drawing challenges.

However, as an adaptation, there are changes. The impactful rain dance spread in the original work lacks a close-up in the film, and the rhythm is delayed during fixed camera shots, which results in a perceived lack of impact. Honestly, when the most shocking spread turned into a smooth but brief card, my first reaction was, "Where's my picture?" But then I realized this is the difference between the film and the comic's focus. One emphasizes delicacy, while the other emphasizes expressiveness.

The original has sparse dialogue, so the film might add appropriate sounds to convey emotions in certain scenes. But apart from the argument between the two characters about their future choices, there seems to be no other emotionally heightened moments. Instead of voice acting, the film uses silence (accompanied by music) to transition through scenes of running, laughing, or potential vocal outbursts. This gives the film a gentle and soothing tone, similar to the original but with a different effect due to the absence of music and voice acting, which in comics speeds up reading and creates a sense of close-up, enhancing impact.

I recall a review saying that "Look Back" is a gentle blow from Fujimoto Tatsuki's heart. If the original is more of a "heartfelt blow," then the film is more "heartfelt and gentle." Understanding this difference in focus makes it easier to comprehend the handling of close-up scenes and fixed camera shots. Comics and animations are different; one is a slice, the other a stack of slices, with the addition of various art forms, making animations capable of expressing much more. Animations can convey not just a glance but a blink, a breath, the weight of the air, the tremble in the chest. Animations should be more delicate, I've always thought. Therefore, judging animations by the standards of comics might be unfair. A photo can never capture the beauty of a person seen in real life. A still image cannot interpret the motion of a living picture. This is what makes "animation" so precious.

Fujimoto Tatsuki and Oshiya Kiyotaka have crafted a love letter to comic and animation creators, as well as to the countless supporters who push them forward. This film is undoubtedly the animation of the year.
